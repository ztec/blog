## Society

*Le code a changé*, 3 episodes about potentially catastrophic legislative changes.

 - [Omnibus : la directive européenne inquiétante 1/3](https://www.radiofrance.fr/franceinter/podcasts/le-code-a-change/omnibus-1-7036685) (Podcast) (fr)
 - [L'Amérique, colon digital 2/3](https://www.radiofrance.fr/franceinter/podcasts/le-code-a-change/omnibus-2-1367650) (Podcast) (fr)
 - [Histoire d'un lanceur d'alerte 3/3](https://www.radiofrance.fr/franceinter/podcasts/le-code-a-change/omnibus-3-4975946) (Podcast) (fr)

Nothing new under the sun regarding the digital vassalization of Europe, but good reminders and connections with the past.
I really like the comparison between the ToS we accept without reading, and European colonizers upon their arrival in the Americas who had
indigenous people read and accept long, impossible-to-read texts.

Concretely, Omnibus aims to "simplify" regulations that are clearly not being enforced. Honestly, saying that the GDPR is complicated
is truly an asshole take. It's one of the simplest and shortest pieces of regulation out there. No, you have to be an asshole to
try to overcomplicate the thing in order to bypass it. Because if you're not an asshole towards your customers or your visitors/users, then
the GDPR isn't hard, and you don't need cookie pop-ups or anything like that. And when, in the podcast, he keeps saying the GDPR has flaws, honestly
he's only talking about these asshole moves, not real flaws. Disappointing that he repeats it so many times for free. It's not the GDPR that imposes banners, that forces dark-patterns
and organizes the deception of visitors/customers. It's the companies, for the sake of surveillance capitalism. They participate in it, like collaborators!

On top of that, the "simplifications" they want to make won't help our companies at all. It's only for the American tech giants, to leave
more room for workarounds, and add even more legislative instability.

 - [Sondage de DPO à l'appui, noyb affirme que la simplification RGPD est à côté de la plaque](https://next.ink/227599/sondage-de-dpo-a-lappui-noyb-affirme-que-la-simplification-rgpd-est-a-cote-de-la-plaque/) (fr)

Speaking of surveillance capitalism and fascism, I learn that Microsoft is being a jerk again (oh surprise), and that lots of games
are getting striked on Steam, probably unjustly too. Personally I'm opposed to the concept of intellectual property protection (a topic for another day), so it
makes me even angrier that anyone would prevent other people from creating under the pretense that it's "too" close to what they own (and didn't even create)!
In short, Microsoft is pulling a Nintendo, as usual.

## AI

Yeah, there isn't a week without some scary novelty/reflection/analysis about AI. The bubble is still here, it's wobbling a bit but
with the consequences of agent orange, hard to see it bursting anytime soon.

 - [The job losses are real — but the AI excuse is fake](https://pivot-to-ai.com/2026/01/29/the-job-losses-are-real-but-the-ai-excuse-is-fake/)

This first article is a bit of a reminder that AI doesn't necessarily (understatement?) create new behaviors. Capitalism does just fine on its own.
AI is just the trendy excuse to try to mask reality. Good article that reminds us of all that.

 - [The Eternal Promise: A History of Attempts to Eliminate Programmers](https://www.ivanturkovic.com/2026/01/22/history-software-simplification-cobol-ai-hype/)

In the vein of "AI isn't revolutionizing anything", a good reminder of the facts about our history in software development, before the name even existed.
It's both reassuring and scary. Because despite everything, the countless articles promising the elimination of the developer role are omnipresent and weigh on morale. This one counterbalances a bit.
However, once again capitalism is killing us even more.
I like the optimistic side of the analysis. Each step leads to more and better despite everything, without
erasing what exists or the past, consolidating it further instead.

 - [Open Source After the Extraction](https://julien.danjou.info/blog/open-source-after-the-extraction/)
 - [GitHub Is Thinking About Killing Pull Requests ](https://julien.danjou.info/blog/github-is-thinking-about-killing-pull-requests/)

The harmful consequences are very much here and visible. The revolution is causing harm, to people and to projects. Open source collaboration, as we know it,
may be living its last moments. Either we manage to counterbalance the effects of AI on the commons, or we'll have to find
a new way to create these commons while coexisting with the consequences of AI. For now the reactions are more about protection and closing ranks.
Examples abound. The closing of contributions, and of certain [bug bounties](https://daniel.haxx.se/blog/2026/01/26/the-end-of-the-curl-bug-bounty/) are good ones.
Some try to manage differently, like [Gnome redirecting almost all its git traffic to GitHub to benefit from free hosting](https://www.phoronix.com/news/GNOME-GitHub-GitLab-Redirect). (without leaving its own forge)
But how long will these "free" solutions remain viable (assuming they even are right now)?

 - [An AI Agent Published a Hit Piece on Me](https://theshamblog.com/an-ai-agent-published-a-hit-piece-on-me/)

And to top it all off, terminator AIs are creeping in. And basically the only answer we get to why? is an Oops, sorry. That wasn't my intention!
It would be good to revisit the basics then. Here are a few videos that are NOT recent on the topic:

 - [Deadly Truth of General AI? - Computerphile](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tcdVC4e6EV4) (YouTube)
 - [General AI Won't Want You To Fix its Code - Computerphile](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4l7Is6vOAOA) (YouTube)
 - [AI "Stop Button" Problem - Computerphile](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3TYT1QfdfsM) (YouTube)

The [full playlist](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tlS5Y2vm02c&list=PLzH6n4zXuckquVnQ0KlMDxyT5YE-sA8Ps&index=1)

And when AI isn't doing harm online, its infrastructure takes care of it in the real world. There's a topic that seems to fly under
the radar. The question of noise pollution, and specifically infrasound and its effects on health.
This video explores the subject, and the rigor as well as the scientific references make the problem credible.

 - [Datacenters Behaving Like Acoustic Weapons](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_bP80DEAbuo) (YouTube)

This challenges my enthusiasm for datacenters built in urban areas. Which is a good idea on paper,
especially to use the heat produced for heating and hot water. Reducing heat losses by
putting them to use. But has the question of noise pollution, even inaudible, been taken into account?

To sum up, here are the concerns I know of regarding datacenters:
 - Energy consumption is becoming a problem (because of AI)
 - Water consumption is sometimes a concern (a disgrace, cf one of my previous Loots)
 - Infrasound production is potentially a concern that turns them into an AoE (Area of Effect)

Worth keeping tabs on, if I find more info on this.

testimony on the use of LLMs and what they make possible. To take a step back on the topic.
